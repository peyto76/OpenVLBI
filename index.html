What is VLBI?

Very Long Baseline Interferometry (VLBI) is a technique in which multiple radio telescopes observe the same source simultaneously. The signals are recorded with precise timestamps and later cross-correlated to produce interference fringes. The angular resolution is set not by any single dish size, but by the longest baseline — the distance between the furthest pair of stations. With stations on opposite sides of Earth, the effective aperture is ~12,000 km.

At 10 GHz (wavelength λ = 3 cm), the theoretical angular resolution is θ = λ/B ≈ 3cm / 12,000km ≈ 0.5 microarcseconds — roughly 100,000× sharper than the human eye and ~20,000× sharper than Hubble in the optical.

Why 10 GHz?

10 GHz (X-band) is a sweet spot for amateur VLBI: dishes large enough to achieve useful gain (1.2 m gives ~32 dBi) are affordable and mechanically manageable. The atmosphere is largely transparent at 10 GHz unlike at mm-wavelengths. Satellite TV equipment provides cheap starting points for feeds and LNAs. Professional VLBI networks (VLBA, EVN) regularly work at X-band, so calibrator source databases are rich.

Hardware Build Guide

Step 1 — Dish
Choose a surplus parabolic dish, 1.2 m minimum (1.8 m preferred). Solid dishes outperform mesh at 10 GHz. Surface accuracy should be better than λ/20 ≈ 1.5 mm RMS. Former DirecTV or ViaSat dishes can be converted with a custom feed.
Step 2 — Feed & LNA
A scalar ring feed or corrugated horn matched to f/D ~ 0.4 typical of offset dishes. Follow with a low-noise amplifier (NF ≤ 1.5 dB, gain ≥ 25 dB) as close to the feed as physically possible. Kuhne Electronic and Minikits sell suitable units.
Step 3 — Downconverter
Convert 10 GHz to a lower IF (e.g., 0.5–2.5 GHz) using a mixer driven from your GPSDO-referenced LO. Commercial PLL synthesizers (ADF4351) locked to the 10 MHz GPSDO reference work well. Maintain phase stability — use low-loss semi-rigid coax.
Step 4 — Az-El mount
A motorized az-el mount capable of 5°/s slew and ≤0.1° RMS pointing. Yaesu G-5500 or equivalent az-el rotor. Control via computer using GPredict or custom pointing scripts. Calibrate using solar drift scans or a bright point source.
Step 5 — GPSDO
GPS-disciplined oscillator providing 10 MHz reference and 1 PPS signal. See the GPSDO & Timing section for detailed guidance. Mount GPS antenna with clear sky view — avoid multipath from buildings.
Step 6 — SDR / Recorder
A software-defined radio capable of 500 MHz bandwidth, locked to the GPSDO 10 MHz reference. Ettus USRP B210 recommended for starters. Record to fast NVMe drives — you need ≥1 GB/s sustained write speed for 500 MHz × 2-bit sampling.

GPSDO & Timing

Phase-coherent VLBI requires that every station's local oscillator is locked to a common time reference. We use GPS-Disciplined Oscillators (GPSDOs) which discipline a crystal oscillator to the GPS timing signal, achieving stability of ≤1 part-per-billion.

Recommended GPSDOs

Leo Bodnar Mini GPSDOBest value · ~$200
Trimble Thunderbolt ESurplus · excellent stability
Jackson Labs CSACCesium — best performance
DIY: u-blox M8T + VCTCXO~$80 · needs care

Timing requirements

The 1 PPS from the GPSDO triggers VDIF frame timestamps. Absolute time accuracy should be ≤10 ns. Verify your setup by comparing 1PPS to GPS-synchronized NTP — expect <100 ns offset. Your correlator will measure and apply residual clock offsets from the fringe-fitting step, so sub-microsecond timing is often sufficient for detection, but ≤10 ns gives the best fringe SNR.

Tip: Run your GPSDO for at least 30 minutes before an observation to allow the oscillator to thermally stabilize and for the GPS receiver to achieve a solid lock on ≥6 satellites. Log the 1PPS offset throughout — large jumps indicate a problem.

Pointing automation

Use GPredict or our custom pointing scripts to drive your rotor controller. Connect via Hamlib or a custom serial interface. A drift scan on the Sun (always visible, strong) verifies your pointing model before each session.

VDIF Format

VDIF (VLBI Data Interchange Format) is the standard data format for modern VLBI. Each data frame contains a 32-byte header encoding the exact timestamp, station ID, channel, and sample metadata, followed by the raw sample data.

Frame header32 bytes
Frame dataConfigurable (typically 8000 bytes)
Timestamp epochGPS seconds since J2000.0
Sample encoding2-bit offset binary (VLBI standard)
Thread conceptOne per IF channel / polarization
Critical: The station ID in the VDIF header must match your registered station code exactly. Mismatched IDs will cause your baseline to be rejected by the correlator.

Correlation Pipeline

After all stations upload their VDIF files, the DiFX software correlator computes cross-correlations between every pair of stations (baselines). This produces visibility data — complex numbers representing the correlated amplitude and phase at each baseline, frequency channel, and time integration.

1. Data validation
MD5 checksums verified. VDIF headers parsed. Station IDs checked against registration database.
2. Clock offset estimation
Fringes found on bright calibrator (3C 273). Sub-nanosecond clock offsets measured per station.
3. Delay model (CALC)
Geometric delays computed for each baseline using station coordinates, Earth rotation, and source position.
4. Cross-correlation (DiFX)
FX correlator architecture. Each baseline processed independently on separate CPU cores.
5. Fringe fitting (AIPS FRING)
Residual delays and rates fitted. Bandpass calibration applied.
6. Imaging (CLEAN)
Fourier inversion and deconvolution produce the radio image. Published as FITS file.

Radio Imaging

The correlated visibilities populate the Fourier (uv) plane. The radio image is the inverse Fourier transform of this sampled plane. Because we only sample discrete points (our baselines), we must deconvolve the "dirty beam" from the "dirty image" using the CLEAN algorithm.

Recommended software

AIPS (NRAO)Proven, industry standard
CASA (NRAO)Python-based, modern
WSCleanFast, good for large fields
DifmapClassic VLBI imaging
All our published results include the raw visibility FITS file so you can image them yourself with any of the above tools.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need an amateur radio license?
For receive-only operation, no license is required in most jurisdictions. We recommend a General class license or equivalent for full participation and to join our coordination nets.
What if I can't afford a USRP B210?
LimeSDR ($300) or a custom FPGA recorder are alternatives. The key requirement is a stable external 10 MHz reference input and 500 MHz IF bandwidth. Some members have used modified RTLSDR setups for initial testing, though bandwidth is limited.
How much internet bandwidth do I need to upload?
A 2-hour session at 500 MHz, 2-bit produces ~150 GB. Upload at 100 Mbps takes ~3.5 hours. We accept post-session uploads — you don't need to upload in real time. Overnight upload is typical.
What if my fringe is not detected?
Common causes: incorrect GPSDO lock, pointing error >1 beamwidth, VDIF timestamp mismatch, or wrong station coordinates. We help diagnose via Discord. The calibration scan against 3C 273 (very bright, always fringes) is the first diagnostic tool.
Can stations in the southern hemisphere join?
Absolutely — southern stations are extremely valuable for north-south baseline coverage and for observing southern targets like Sgr A* and Centaurus A. We actively recruit stations in Chile, South Africa, Australia, and New Zealand.
