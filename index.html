PIPELINE OVERVIEW
Step 1 -- Data ingestion
All station VDIF files checksummed (MD5), station IDs validated against registration database.
Step 2 -- Clock offset estimation
Fringes detected on bright calibrator (3C 273). Sub-nanosecond clock offsets measured per station.
Step 3 -- Delay model (CALC11)
Geometric delays computed for each baseline using station positions, Earth rotation, and source coordinates.
Step 4 -- Cross-correlation (DiFX 2.8)
FX correlator architecture. Each baseline processed independently. Produces visibility data per channel per integration.
Step 5 -- Fringe fitting (AIPS FRING)
Residual delays and rates fitted. Bandpass calibration applied.
Step 6 -- Imaging (CLEAN)
Fourier inversion and deconvolution produce the radio image. Published as FITS file under CC-BY 4.0.

Technical reference

What is VLBI?

Very Long Baseline Interferometry (VLBI) is a technique in which multiple radio telescopes observe the same source simultaneously. Signals are recorded with precise timestamps, then cross-correlated to produce interference fringes. Angular resolution is set by the longest baseline -- the distance between the furthest pair of stations.

At 10 GHz (lambda = 3 cm), a 10,000 km baseline gives θ = lambda/B ≈ 0.6 microarcseconds -- roughly 80,000× sharper than the human eye and ~80× sharper than Hubble in the optical.

Why 10 GHz (X-band)?

The atmosphere is largely transparent. Dish gains are achievable with affordable hardware (1.2 m gives ~32 dBi). Professional calibrator source databases are rich at X-band. Modified satellite TV LNBs provide a low-cost starting point.

Hardware Build Guide

Step 1 -- Dish
Choose a surplus parabolic dish, 1.2 m minimum (1.8 m preferred). Surface accuracy should be better than lambda/20 ≈ 1.5 mm RMS. Former DirecTV or surplus C-band dishes can be converted.
Step 2 -- Feed & LNA
A scalar ring feed or corrugated horn matched to f/D ~ 0.4. Follow with LNA (NF ≤ 1.5 dB, gain ≥ 25 dB) as close to the feed as possible. Kuhne Electronic and Minikits sell suitable units.
Step 3 -- Downconverter
Convert 10 GHz to IF (0.5-2.5 GHz) using a mixer driven from a GPSDO-referenced LO. ADF4351 PLL synthesizer locked to GPSDO 10 MHz works well. Use low-loss semi-rigid coax.
Step 4 -- Az-El Mount
Motorized mount capable of 5°/s slew and ≤0.1° RMS pointing. Yaesu G-5500 or equivalent. Control via GPredict or custom scripts. Calibrate with solar drift scans.
Step 5 -- GPSDO
GPS-disciplined oscillator providing 10 MHz reference and 1 PPS signal. Mount GPS antenna with clear sky view -- avoid multipath. Run 30 min before observation.
Step 6 -- SDR Recorder
SDR locked to GPSDO 10 MHz reference. Ettus USRP B210 recommended. Record to fast NVMe -- need ≥1 GB/s sustained write for 500 MHz 2-bit sampling (~150 GB/hr).

GPSDO & Timing

Phase-coherent VLBI requires every station's oscillator locked to a common time reference. We use GPS-Disciplined Oscillators achieving ≤1 ppb stability. The 1 PPS signal triggers VDIF frame timestamps to ≤10 ns absolute accuracy.

Recommended GPSDOs

Leo Bodnar Mini GPSDOBest value · ~$200
Trimble Thunderbolt ESurplus · excellent stability
Jackson Labs CSACCesium -- best performance
DIY: u-blox M8T + VCTCXO~$80 · needs care
Run your GPSDO for at least 30 minutes before an observation and log 1PPS offset throughout. Large jumps indicate a problem. Verify against GPS-synchronized NTP -- expect <100 ns offset.

VDIF Format

VDIF (VLBI Data Interchange Format) is the standard data format for modern VLBI. Each frame has a 32-byte header encoding the exact timestamp, station ID, channel, and sample metadata, followed by raw sample data.

Frame header32 bytes
Timestamp epochGPS seconds since J2000.0
Sample encoding2-bit offset binary
Thread conceptOne per polarization / IF channel
Critical: The station ID in the VDIF header must exactly match your registered callsign. Mismatched IDs cause baseline rejection.

Correlation Pipeline

The DiFX software correlator computes cross-correlations between every pair of stations producing complex visibility data -- correlated amplitude and phase at each baseline, frequency channel, and time integration. This populates the uv-plane for imaging.

1 -- Validate
MD5 checksums verified. VDIF headers parsed. Station IDs matched to registration database.
2 -- Clock offsets
Fringes found on 3C 273. Sub-nanosecond clock offsets measured per station using AIPS FRING.
3 -- Delay model
Geometric delays computed using CALC11 for each baseline accounting for Earth rotation.
4 -- Cross-correlate
FX correlator computes visibility data for each baseline independently across all CPU cores.
5 -- Image
CLEAN deconvolution in AIPS or CASA produces the final radio image. Published as FITS.

FAQ

Do I need an amateur radio license?
For receive-only operation, no license is required in most jurisdictions. A General class or equivalent is recommended for coordination net participation.
What if I can't afford a USRP B210?
LimeSDR (~$300) is an alternative. The key requirements are a stable 10 MHz external reference input and at least 500 MHz IF bandwidth. Custom FPGA recorders are also welcome.
How much internet do I need to upload?
A 2-hour session at 500 MHz 2-bit produces ~150 GB. At 100 Mbps upload speed that takes ~3.5 hours. Overnight upload is typical -- you don't need to upload in real time.
What if my fringe isn't detected?
Common causes: incorrect GPSDO lock, pointing error larger than one beamwidth, VDIF timestamp mismatch, or wrong station coordinates. The 3C 273 calibration scan is the first diagnostic -- it is very bright and should always produce fringes on any working baseline.
Can southern hemisphere stations join?
Absolutely -- and they are extremely valuable for north-south baseline coverage and for observing southern targets like Sgr A* and Centaurus A. We actively recruit stations in Chile, South Africa, Australia, and New Zealand.
